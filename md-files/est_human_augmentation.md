## Human Augmentation and Body Modification

At what point does a person with technology embedded in their body stop being fully human and start being something else? It is a question that science fiction has explored for decades, and one that is becoming increasingly relevant as the technologies of augmentation move from the screen to the operating table.

### What Is Human Augmentation?

Human augmentation encompasses a broad spectrum of technologies that enhance or extend the capabilities of the human body. At one end are familiar devices like hearing aids, prosthetic limbs, and pacemakers, technologies that restore lost function. At the other end lies a vision of deep integration between biology and technology: implanted sensors, powered exoskeletons, artificial organs, and cybernetic systems that grant abilities beyond what any unmodified human possesses.

The boundary between therapeutic and enhancement is often blurry. A prosthetic leg that allows an amputee to walk is therapy. A prosthetic leg that allows a runner to outperform athletes with biological legs raises very different questions. And as these technologies improve, the line between restoring what was lost and adding what was never there will become increasingly difficult to draw.

### How the Book Explores It

*Films from the Future* (Chapter 7) uses the 1995 anime film *Ghost in the Shell* to explore human augmentation taken to its extreme. The film is set in a future where cybernetic enhancement is widespread and people routinely replace biological components with technological ones. At the center of the story is Major Kusanagi, a law enforcement officer whose body is almost entirely cybernetic. Only her brain, her "ghost," remains biological.

This extreme level of augmentation raises the question that drives the film: if nearly everything about you has been replaced by technology, what is it that makes you "you"? Kusanagi's struggle with her identity reflects a deeper anxiety about what happens to our sense of self when the boundary between person and machine becomes permeable.

The book connects this fictional scenario to real-world developments. It discusses Elon Musk's Neuralink venture, advances in powered prosthetics, and the growing community of biohackers who are already implanting magnets, RFID chips, and other devices in their bodies. These are early steps, but they point toward a future where the human body becomes a platform for technological integration.

### Where Things Stand Today

Augmentation technologies are advancing rapidly. Prosthetic limbs controlled by neural signals are becoming more sophisticated. Cochlear implants have restored hearing to hundreds of thousands of people. Powered exoskeletons are being developed to help paralyzed individuals walk. And on the experimental frontier, researchers are working on artificial retinas, brain-computer interfaces, and implanted drug-delivery systems.

Outside the medical mainstream, a subculture of biohackers and "grinders" is experimenting with do-it-yourself body modification, implanting devices that can sense magnetic fields, unlock doors, or store personal data. These grassroots experiments are crude compared to clinical technologies, but they signal a growing appetite for merging body and machine on personal terms.

### Why It Matters

Human augmentation raises profound questions about identity, equity, and the meaning of human experience. If some people can afford to enhance their vision, strength, or cognitive function through technology, and others cannot, the resulting inequality goes beyond economics into something more fundamental.

There are also questions about autonomy and consent. If augmentation becomes expected in certain professions, such as military service or high-performance athletics, individuals may face pressure to modify their bodies in ways they would not choose freely. And as augmentation technologies become more powerful, the social definition of "normal" human capability may shift, leaving unaugmented individuals at a disadvantage.

The book frames augmentation as one piece of a larger puzzle about what it means to be human in an age of technological transformation, a theme that connects to cloning, artificial intelligence, and the broader question of how we define dignity and rights in a world where the boundaries of the human are no longer fixed.

## Further Reading

- [Being Human in an Augmented Future — Moviegoer's Guide to the Future (Future of Being Human)](https://www.futureofbeinghuman.com/p/being-human-in-an-augmented-future) — Andrew Maynard uses *Ghost in the Shell* to explore what happens when the boundary between person and machine becomes permeable, examining the real-world trajectory of augmentation technologies and the profound identity questions they raise.
- [The Future of Being Human in 2024 — Andrew Maynard (Future of Being Human)](https://www.futureofbeinghuman.com/p/the-future-of-being-human-in-2024) — Maynard lays out the converging technological fronts — gene editing, brain-machine interfaces, synthetic biology, nanoscale science — arguing we may be approaching a tipping point where technologies shift from augmenting who we are to fundamentally changing what we are.
- [UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Neurotechnology (November 2025)](https://www.unesco.org/en/ethics-neurotech) — The first global normative framework for neurotechnology ethics, calling for neural data to be classified as sensitive personal data, warning against workplace neuromonitoring, and recommending prohibition of any direct or indirect pressure on individuals to undergo neurotechnological enhancement.
- [Beyond Human Limits: The Ethical, Social, and Regulatory Implications of Human Enhancement — Gerardi & Xinaris, *Frontiers in Medicine* (July 2025)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2025.1595213/full) — A comprehensive open-access review examining motor neuroprostheses, transcranial magnetic stimulation, and gene therapy, directly tackling the therapy-vs-enhancement distinction and discussing the risks of enhancement technologies deepening social inequalities.
- [Fine-Tuned Brain-Computer Interface Makes Prosthetic Limbs Feel More Real — UChicago Medicine (January 2025)](https://www.uchicagomedicine.org/forefront/biological-sciences-articles/2025/january/bionic-hand-sensation) — Two landmark studies demonstrate that brain microstimulation can let bionic hand users feel edges, shapes, and motion across prosthetic fingers — users could identify letters traced on fingertips and control a steering wheel, moving prosthetics from functional tools toward sensory integration with the body.
- [Real-World Helper Exoskeletons Just Got Closer to Reality — Georgia Tech / *Science Robotics* (November 2025)](https://news.gatech.edu/news/2025/11/19/real-world-helper-exoskeletons-just-got-closer-reality) — Researchers developed an AI system that rapidly generates exoskeleton controllers from existing movement data, eliminating the costly lab calibration that has kept wearable robots impractical — a key step toward exoskeletons moving from clinical rehabilitation into everyday consumer use.
- [A Bionic Knee Integrated into Tissue Can Restore Natural Movement — MIT News (2025)](https://news.mit.edu/2025/bionic-knee-integrated-into-tissue-can-restore-natural-movement-0710) — MIT researchers developed a prosthetic knee that fuses directly with bone and muscle tissue, enabling neural control and a sense of embodiment. Users reported the limb feeling like part of their body — precisely the blurring of human and machine that *Ghost in the Shell* anticipates.
- [What Should Cardiac Patients Know About Device Cybersecurity Prior to Implantation? — *AMA Journal of Ethics* (2021)](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/what-should-cardiac-patients-know-about-device-cybersecurity-prior-implantation/2021-09) — When a wirelessly connected defibrillator is implanted in your chest, who controls it? This article explores the cybersecurity, consent, and autonomy questions raised by cardiac implantable devices — making vivid the book's point that augmentation is not a future concern but a present one.
