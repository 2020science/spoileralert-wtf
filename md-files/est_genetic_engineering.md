## Genetic Engineering and Gene Editing

Of all the technologies explored in *Films from the Future*, genetic engineering may be the one with the most far-reaching implications. It is the ability to read, rewrite, and redesign the DNA-based code that underpins every living organism on Earth. And thanks to tools like CRISPR-Cas9, this ability is advancing faster than almost anyone anticipated.

### What Is Genetic Engineering?

Genetic engineering is the deliberate modification of an organism's genetic material. At its most basic level, it involves identifying specific genes, understanding what they do, and then altering them to change how an organism develops or functions. This can mean inserting a gene from one species into another, deleting a gene that causes disease, or fine-tuning genetic sequences to produce a desired trait.

The field has existed in various forms since the 1970s, but the arrival of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing in the early 2010s changed everything. CRISPR allows scientists to target specific locations in the genome with unprecedented precision, making edits that would have taken years using older methods. It is faster, cheaper, and more accurate than any previous gene-editing tool, and it has democratized genetic engineering in ways that are both exciting and concerning.

### How the Book Explores It

Genetic engineering surfaces across multiple chapters of *Films from the Future*, but it takes center stage in the discussion of *Jurassic Park* (Chapter 2). In the film, the fictional InGen corporation uses genetic engineering to reconstruct dinosaur DNA and grow living creatures from it. The science is Hollywood spectacle, but the underlying principle, that we can manipulate genetic code to create organisms with specific traits, is grounded in reality.

The book also explores genetic engineering through *Inferno* (Chapter 11), where the technology is turned toward a darker purpose: engineering a pathogen designed to reduce the human population. And in the discussion of *The Day After Tomorrow* (Chapter 12), the broader implications of genetic modification for agriculture and ecosystems come into play.

Throughout these discussions, the book returns to a core tension: the extraordinary power of genetic engineering and the difficulty of predicting what happens when we release our modifications into the complex web of living systems.

### Where Things Stand Today

CRISPR and related gene-editing technologies are now being used across an enormous range of applications. In medicine, clinical trials are underway for CRISPR-based treatments for sickle cell disease, certain cancers, and genetic disorders. In agriculture, gene-edited crops are being developed to resist pests, tolerate drought, and deliver improved nutrition. In conservation, researchers are exploring how gene editing might help endangered species adapt to rapidly changing environments.

But the technology has also generated serious concerns. In 2018, a Chinese scientist announced that he had edited the genomes of twin human embryos, an act that was widely condemned by the scientific community as premature and reckless. The incident underscored the gap between what is technically possible and what is ethically acceptable, and it triggered a global conversation about where to draw the line on human genetic modification.

### Why It Matters

Genetic engineering sits at the intersection of enormous promise and significant risk. It could eliminate hereditary diseases, feed a growing global population, and protect biodiversity. But it could also be used to create biological weapons, exacerbate social inequalities if access is uneven, or produce ecological disruptions that we cannot foresee or reverse.

The challenge, as *Films from the Future* repeatedly emphasizes, is not the technology itself but how we choose to develop and use it. Genetic engineering demands governance frameworks that are as sophisticated as the science, and it requires broad societal input into decisions that will affect everyone. The era of a handful of experts making these choices behind closed doors is, or should be, over.

- [Jurassic World: Dominion's Social Commentary Is Worth Heeding — Andrew Maynard (Future of Being Human, 2022)](https://www.futureofbeinghuman.com/p/jurassic-park-dominion-may-fall-short-on-the-science-but-its-social-commentary-is-worth-heeding-fc48c9344e7d) — Maynard examines how the latest Jurassic installment carries a cautionary tale about technological hubris that is increasingly relevant as our ability to manipulate DNA accelerates, framing the film as a prompt for thinking about socially responsible innovation.
- [Vertex's CRISPR Treatment for Sickle Cell Disease Hits Unexpected Roadblock — STAT News (February 2026)](https://www.statnews.com/2026/02/05/vertex-crispr-sickle-cell-treatment-casgevy-faces-rollout-bottleneck/) — More than two years after approval, only about 60 patients have received Casgevy — the first approved CRISPR gene therapy. This investigative report highlights the gap between laboratory breakthroughs and equitable patient access that is central to the book's themes.
- [International Call for a 10-Year Moratorium on Heritable Human Genome Editing — ISCT/ARM/ASGCT, *Cytotherapy* (May 2025)](https://www.isct-cytotherapy.org/article/S1465-3249(25)00718-2/fulltext) — Three leading gene therapy professional organizations call for a moratorium until at least 2035, arguing that alternatives to germline editing exist, safety cannot be demonstrated within a single generation, and premature clinical application risks undermining public trust in genetic medicine.
- [FDA Illuminates New Approval Pathway for Bespoke Gene Editing Therapies — Fierce Biotech (2025)](https://www.fiercebiotech.com/biotech/fda-illuminates-new-approval-pathway-bespoke-gene-therapies) — Covers the FDA's new "plausible mechanism" framework for approving personalized CRISPR therapies, prompted by the case of Baby KJ who received a custom in vivo CRISPR treatment for a rare metabolic disorder in just six months — a significant shift in how regulators are adapting to individualized gene editing.
- [Regulatory Challenges and Global Trade Implications of Genome Editing in Agriculture — *Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology* (2025)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1609110/full) — A peer-reviewed analysis of divergent regulatory approaches to agricultural gene editing worldwide — the EU's two-category system, the UK's Precision Breeding Act (effective November 2025), and more flexible frameworks in Latin America and Asia — raising questions about trade, innovation equity, and food security.
- [CRISPR Democracy: Gene Editing and the Need for Inclusive Deliberation — Jasanoff, Hurlbut & Saha, *Issues in Science and Technology*](https://issues.org/crispr-democracy-gene-editing-inclusive-deliberation/) — An influential essay arguing that CRISPR decisions should not be left to scientists alone but require broad democratic deliberation about which futures are worth pursuing, critiquing the narrow technical framing of gene editing governance and arguing that public concerns reflect legitimate moral reasoning.
