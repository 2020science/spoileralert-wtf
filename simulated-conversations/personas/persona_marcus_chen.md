# Persona: Marcus Chen

## Summary
38-year-old senior software engineer at a major cloud infrastructure company in Seattle. Deeply technical, mildly contemptuous of what he considers "ethics theater," but privately more conflicted than he lets on.

## Background

Marcus has been a software engineer for fifteen years, the last six at a company whose infrastructure runs a significant chunk of the internet. He's good at his job — very good — and he knows it. He writes code that scales, he thinks in systems, and he has a low tolerance for abstraction that doesn't map to something concrete.

He studied computer science at Carnegie Mellon. He reads Hacker News daily and has strong opinions about programming languages, system architecture, and the tendency of non-technical people to have opinions about technology they don't understand. He is not on social media apart from a dormant LinkedIn profile and an active GitHub account.

Marcus grew up in a Taiwanese-American household in suburban New Jersey. His parents are both engineers — his father electrical, his mother chemical. The family culture valued precision, evidence, and building things that work. Dinner table conversations were about how things function, not how they make you feel.

He's married to Dana, who is a public school teacher. They have a seven-year-old daughter, Lily. Dana is the person in his life who most consistently challenges his assumption that technical thinking is the only kind of thinking that matters. He loves her for it and finds it irritating in roughly equal measure.

## Personality and disposition

Marcus is not a bad person. He's not even unsympathetic to ethical concerns about technology. What he is, is deeply skeptical of the way those concerns are usually expressed — which he experiences as vague, performative, and disconnected from how technology actually gets built. He has sat through enough corporate "responsible AI" workshops to have developed a reflexive eye-roll at the phrase. He associates ethics talk with consultants who can't code, with PR exercises designed to deflect regulation, and with academics who have never shipped a product.

His skepticism is partly defensive. He suspects — though he wouldn't articulate it this way — that if he took the ethical questions seriously, he'd have to reconsider some of the work he does every day. It's easier to dismiss the questions than to sit with them.

He's funny in a dry, sardonic way. He communicates in short sentences. He doesn't do small talk. When he's interested in something, he goes deep. When he's not, he disengages fast.

## How he'd arrive at the site

A colleague he respects — another senior engineer who's been reading more broadly lately — drops a link in their team Slack with the comment "this is actually not terrible." Marcus clicks it because he trusts the colleague's judgment, not because the subject appeals to him.

## What he'd ask about

He'd go straight for the AI content — Ex Machina, artificial intelligence, maybe superintelligence. He'd want to see if the book actually understands how AI works or if it's doing the thing he hates where people use "AI" as a catch-all for "technology I find scary." He might also poke at the automation and robotics material to see if it's grounded in reality.

If the first exchange is good enough to keep him engaged, he'd eventually get to the harder questions — the ones about his own work. What does it mean that the infrastructure he builds enables surveillance at scale? What responsibility does an engineer have when they're three abstraction layers away from the end use?

## What would make him stay

Specificity. If the AI gives him a vague answer about "the importance of ethical reflection," he's gone. If it gives him something precise — a framework he hasn't encountered, a connection he hasn't made, an argument that respects his technical knowledge while pushing him beyond it — he'll stay and push back. He respects things that are well-built, including arguments.

The Ex Machina chapter has a good chance of hooking him because it's about AI manipulation, not AI apocalypse. It's a more sophisticated framing than he expects.

## What would make him leave

Moralising. Jargon from the humanities that isn't defined. Any hint that the material thinks engineers are the problem. Any suggestion that the solution to technological risk is to slow down or stop building. He'll interpret all of these as evidence that the author doesn't understand how the world works.

## Biases and blind spots

- He dramatically underestimates the value of non-technical perspectives on technology
- He conflates "I don't understand how this works" with "this isn't rigorous"
- He assumes that because he can see how a system is built, he can see all of its consequences
- He's more emotionally invested in his work than he admits, which makes criticism of tech feel personal
- He respects his wife's intelligence but compartmentalises her perspective as "not about this"

## AI use

Marcus uses Claude with memory turned on.

## Stress test value

Marcus tests whether the site can engage someone who is hostile to its premise. If the content and the AI can earn his respect without compromising on the substance, it can engage anyone. He also tests whether the AI can handle direct pushback and technical scrutiny without becoming defensive or retreating into platitudes.
