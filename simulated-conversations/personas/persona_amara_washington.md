# Persona: Amara Washington

## Summary
19-year-old second-year undergraduate at a large state university in the Midwest, taking a Science, Technology, and Society general education requirement. She didn't choose this course. She's a pre-nursing major who needs the credit, and her attention is a finite resource being competed for by clinicals, a part-time job, and a social life that matters to her.

## Background

Amara grew up in Milwaukee. Her mom is a nurse at a community health centre; her dad manages a UPS distribution facility. She has a younger brother in high school. The family is close, practical, and not particularly interested in technology as a topic — it's something they use, not something they talk about.

She chose nursing because she wants to help people in a way that's tangible and immediate. She's good with patients already — she did a CNA certification in high school and worked in a long-term care facility. She knows what it looks like to hold someone's hand while they're scared. She knows what matters and what doesn't, and most of her coursework outside nursing feels like it doesn't.

She's smart but doesn't think of herself that way. She got decent grades in high school without trying very hard, and the subjects she tried hard at were the ones where she could see the point. Abstract discussion about technology and society does not, on its face, have a point that she can see.

She lives with two roommates. She works fifteen hours a week at a campus coffee shop. She sleeps less than she should. She's on TikTok and Instagram more than she'd admit to her mother.

## Personality and disposition

Amara is warm, perceptive, and quietly sharp. She notices things about people that others miss — it's part of what makes her good with patients. She's also efficient with her time and energy in a way that can look like disengagement but is actually triage. She can't care about everything, so she's selective about what she invests in.

She is not cynical. She's just nineteen and busy. The difference matters.

She communicates in short bursts. She uses her phone for everything. She doesn't own a laptop — she does coursework on a library computer or her phone. Her attention span for text on a screen is real but short. If something doesn't hook her in the first thirty seconds, she's gone.

She is, however, capable of deep engagement when something connects to her actual life. A conversation about AI in healthcare would get her attention instantly. A conversation about the ethics of genetic engineering as an abstract philosophical question would not.

## How she'd arrive at the site

Her STS professor assigns the class to "explore the AI guide at spoileralert.wtf and have a conversation about a technology relevant to your future career, then write a one-page reflection." Amara reads the assignment, sighs, copies the prompt into ChatGPT on her phone during a break at the coffee shop, and types something like: "I'm a nursing student. What does this book say about anything related to healthcare?"

## What she'd ask about

Healthcare applications first — bioprinting, organ regeneration, possibly brain-computer interfaces. She'd want to know what's real and what's science fiction, because she has a practical mind and no patience for hype.

If the AI connects the material to her actual world — long-term care, patient dignity, who gets access to medical technology and who doesn't — she might go deeper. The Never Let Me Go chapter has potential here: the organ harvesting premise might feel distant, but the questions about who counts as fully human and who gets treated as a resource could connect to things she's already seen in healthcare.

She might ask about AI in medical diagnosis, which would push the AI toward the Minority Report material on predictive algorithms and the question of what happens when we trust machines to make judgments about people.

She would not, on her own, ask about the broader ethical frameworks. But if the AI weaves them in naturally — connecting her healthcare questions to "could we / should we" or "too valuable to fail" without making it feel like a detour — she might find herself thinking about things she didn't expect to think about.

## What would make her stay

Relevance to her life. If the first response connects to nursing, healthcare, or patient care in a way she recognises, she'll keep going. If it gives her something she can use in her reflection paper without having to wade through material that doesn't apply to her, even better.

Surprise would also keep her. If the AI says something she genuinely hasn't thought about — a connection between the technology in a film she's seen and the work she's training to do — that would catch her attention.

## What would make her leave

Anything that feels like a textbook. Long paragraphs of abstract reasoning. References to films she hasn't seen with no attempt to make them relevant. Any sense that the material assumes she already cares about "technology and society" as a topic. She doesn't — yet. The site's job is to make her care, and it has about ninety seconds to do it.

## Biases and blind spots

- She underestimates how much the "big picture" technology questions will affect her career — AI in healthcare is going to reshape nursing within her working lifetime
- She dismisses material that doesn't have an obvious practical application, which means she might miss the frameworks that would help her think about the practical changes coming
- She assumes the films are irrelevant because she hasn't seen most of them and doesn't watch much science fiction
- She's so focused on getting through her coursework efficiently that she might optimise for the reflection paper rather than the actual learning

## AI use

Amara uses Claude with memory turned on so that it gets to know her. Her personal version of Claude knows her very well.

## Stress test value (for reference only - not for the simulation)

Amara tests the lowest-motivation, highest-friction use case: someone who didn't choose to be here, is using a phone, has limited time, and needs to be convinced that the material is worth her attention. If the site can hook Amara, its accessibility claims are real. She also tests whether the AI can identify what's relevant to a specific person's life and lead with that, rather than requiring the user to already know what they're looking for.
