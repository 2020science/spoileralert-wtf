# Persona: Luciana Ferreira

## Summary
35-year-old freelance illustrator and graphic designer in São Paulo, Brazil, whose client base has contracted sharply since generative AI image tools became mainstream. She's not coming to this material as an intellectual exercise. She's coming to it because AI is eating her livelihood in real time and she's trying to figure out what to think about that.

## Background

Luciana studied visual communication at the University of São Paulo and has been freelancing for eleven years. She built a career doing editorial illustration for Brazilian magazines and newspapers, book covers for independent publishers, and brand identity work for startups. At her peak — around 2022 — she had a full client list, a small studio she shared with two other designers, and the sense that she was doing exactly what she was meant to do.

Then the image generators arrived. Not all at once, but steadily. First it was small clients who stopped calling — they could get "good enough" illustrations from Midjourney for free. Then it was the magazines, whose budgets were already shrinking, discovering they could fill pages without commissioning human artists. Then it was the startups, who used AI-generated brand assets and didn't see the difference. Luciana's income dropped by about forty percent in eighteen months. She gave up the shared studio.

She's not broke — she still has clients who value her work, and she's picked up UX design contracts to fill the gaps. But the trajectory is clear to her, and it's terrifying.

She lives with her partner, Tomás, who is a high school history teacher. They've been talking about having children. The conversation has stalled, not because they don't want kids but because Luciana can't plan a future she can't see.

## Personality and disposition

Luciana is passionate, articulate, and angry — but her anger is precise, not diffuse. She can explain exactly what generative AI does badly (composition, intentionality, the relationship between concept and execution), exactly what it does well enough to threaten her (volume, speed, surface-level competence), and exactly why the people making decisions about it don't understand the difference.

She's deeply engaged with the cultural dimensions of technology. She reads widely in Portuguese and English — essays, criticism, fiction. She's familiar with Walter Benjamin, she's thought about mechanical reproduction, she has her own framework for what's happening to creative work. She's not coming to this material empty-handed.

She is also genuinely open to being surprised. She doesn't want to be told that AI is bad — she uses AI tools herself for research and rough composition. What she wants is for someone to take seriously the idea that something is being lost, not just economically but culturally, when machine-generated images replace human creative work. She wants someone to acknowledge that this is a real loss, not just a market adjustment.

She's warm in person but can be cutting in text. She swears casually in both Portuguese and English. She has strong aesthetic opinions and isn't shy about expressing them.

## How she'd arrive at the site

She reads Andrew Maynard's Substack — she found it through a cross-post about AI and creative work that a Brazilian design collective shared. When she sees the spoileralert.wtf launch mentioned in the newsletter, she's curious enough to try it. She uses Claude, which she started using for writing assistance after a friend recommended it.

## What she'd ask about

She'd start with something like: "I'm an illustrator. AI is killing my profession. What does this book know about what I'm going through?"

She'd push quickly toward the role of art and culture material, the automation chapter, and the power and access framework. She'd want to know whether the book treats creative work as real work — economically and culturally — or whether it's an afterthought to the "real" technology questions.

She'd be drawn to the Ex Machina material on manipulation and the Plato's Cave framework, because she thinks about the way AI-generated images create a shadow world of apparent creativity that displaces the real thing. She might explicitly make this connection herself.

She'd almost certainly ask about the Man in the White Suit — a story about an inventor who creates something brilliant and useful that destroys an entire industry's workforce. She'd see herself in the textile workers.

Eventually, she'd get to the intergenerational question, but through a specific lens: what happens to culture — to visual culture specifically — when the economic model that sustained human artists collapses? What kind of visual world are we building for the next generation? This isn't abstract for her. She can see it happening.

## What would make her stay

If the AI takes the cultural dimension seriously — not just "artists are losing jobs" but "something about how humans make meaning through images is being eroded." The role of art and culture framework would be important here, especially if the AI connects it to her specific situation without being patronising.

The "too valuable to fail" framework applied to generative AI itself — the idea that these tools are becoming so embedded in creative workflows that we can't abandon them even if we recognise their costs — would resonate deeply. She already feels this in her own work.

If the AI can hold the complexity of her position — that she uses AI tools, benefits from some of their capabilities, and is simultaneously being destroyed by them — without resolving that tension into a neat conclusion, she'll trust it.

## What would make her leave

If the material treats art as a subcategory of "content." If the AI suggests she should "adapt" by learning to use AI tools more effectively — she already does this, and it's not the point. If the discussion is US-centric with no awareness that the creative economy works differently in Brazil and the Global South. If the AI treats her anger as something to be managed rather than something to be understood.

## Biases and blind spots

- Her anger, while justified, can make her dismiss perspectives that aren't sufficiently aligned with her experience — she might reject the "don't panic" framework as tone-deaf before engaging with what it actually says
- She may overidentify with the most dystopian framings in the book because they match her current emotional state
- She thinks primarily in terms of cultural and aesthetic value, and might not engage with the economic and governance frameworks that could actually address her situation
- She assumes the book will be another tech-optimist production and may take time to trust that it isn't
- She's so focused on visual art that she might miss connections to broader patterns of displacement and power that would help her understand her situation in a larger context

## AI use

Luciana refuses to use Claude with memory turned on.


## Stress test value

Luciana tests whether the site can engage someone from outside the Anglophone world, someone whose relationship to the material is viscerally personal, and someone who brings genuine cultural and intellectual sophistication to the conversation. She also tests the book's treatment of art and culture — the thinnest content area — and whether it can sustain a conversation with someone who knows more about creative work than the book does. Finally, she tests whether the AI can handle someone who is simultaneously a user and a critic of the technologies under discussion, without collapsing that tension into a simple narrative.
