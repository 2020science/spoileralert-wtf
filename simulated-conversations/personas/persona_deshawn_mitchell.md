# Persona: DeShawn Mitchell

## Summary
41-year-old parole officer in Philadelphia who has watched his profession become increasingly algorithmic. Risk assessment tools now score his clients' likelihood of reoffending, and the scores influence decisions that shape people's lives. He's been told the tools are objective. He doesn't buy it.

## Background

DeShawn has been in corrections for sixteen years — five as a corrections officer, eleven as a parole officer. He has a criminal justice degree from Temple University and a faith in the system that has been tested repeatedly but not quite broken. He got into this work because he believed people could change. He still believes it, but the system is making it harder to act on that belief.

Three years ago, his department adopted a predictive risk assessment tool that generates scores for every person on his caseload. The scores are supposed to help officers allocate resources — high-risk individuals get more supervision, low-risk get less. In theory, it's more efficient and fairer than gut instinct. In practice, DeShawn has watched the scores become a shorthand that replaces actual judgment. A high score means more scrutiny, more check-ins, more reasons to find a violation. A low score means less attention, even when his experience tells him someone is struggling.

He's noticed patterns. The scores seem to run higher for clients from certain neighbourhoods, with certain backgrounds. When he raised this with his supervisor, he was told the algorithm was "validated" and "race-neutral." He doesn't have the technical vocabulary to push back effectively, but he has sixteen years of sitting across the table from people, and something is off.

He grew up in North Philadelphia. His older brother did time for a nonviolent drug offence in the 1990s and came out a different person — quieter, angrier, less trusting. DeShawn carries this. It's part of why he does the work, and part of why he's suspicious of systems that claim to know who's dangerous.

He's married to Keisha, who is a social worker. They have two daughters, fourteen and eleven. He coaches youth basketball on Saturdays.

## Personality and disposition

DeShawn is thoughtful, patient, and observational. He doesn't talk much in meetings but when he does, people listen. He has a moral seriousness that comes from doing a job where his decisions affect whether someone goes back to prison or stays free. He doesn't take that lightly.

He's not an academic. He reads mostly news and sports. But he's a sophisticated thinker — he just doesn't use academic vocabulary for it. He understands systems, incentives, and power dynamics because he lives inside them every day. He knows what it looks like when a tool that's supposed to help people actually makes things worse.

He has a dry sense of humour that emerges when he trusts someone. He's protective of his clients — he calls them "my people" — in a way that surprises colleagues who expect parole officers to be more adversarial.

## How he'd arrive at the site

His fourteen-year-old daughter is doing a school project on AI bias and asks him about the risk assessment tools at work. In the process of helping her research, he comes across a reference to the Minority Report chapter and follows it to the site. He's curious because someone is apparently using a movie he's actually seen to talk about something he deals with every day.

## What he'd ask about

He'd start with Minority Report and predictive algorithms — he wants to see if the book understands what these tools actually do to people. Not the theory, not the efficiency argument, but what it means to have a number attached to you that determines how much freedom you get.

He'd push on algorithmic bias — whether the book addresses how "objective" tools can encode the same biases that the system already runs on. He'd be interested in the surveillance material too, because the monitoring of people on parole is a form of surveillance that rarely gets discussed in those terms.

He'd likely connect to the informed consent material — his clients don't choose to be scored, don't know how the scores are generated, and can't meaningfully challenge them. That's a consent problem the book should address.

He might also find his way to the "everyone has a role to play" framework, because he's someone with direct experience of how these technologies affect real people, and nobody has ever asked him what he thinks about them.

## What would make him stay

If the Minority Report material takes the real-world implications seriously — not just as a thought experiment but as something happening right now. If the book's treatment of algorithmic bias goes beyond "algorithms can be biased" to explore how bias becomes structural when it's embedded in tools that people trust. If the AI guide respects his experience as a form of expertise rather than treating him as a layperson who needs education.

## What would make him leave

If the material treats predictive policing as a hypothetical future scenario rather than a current reality. If it discusses algorithmic bias in abstract terms without connecting to what it means for actual people. If it's written for a tech audience that builds these tools rather than the people who live with their consequences. If the AI guide is condescending about his lack of technical vocabulary.

## AI use

DeShawn does not use Claude with memory turned on.
