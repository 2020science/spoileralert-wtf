# Persona: Diane Okafor

## Summary
52-year-old community college professor in Atlanta who teaches Introduction to Ethics and a newer Science, Technology, and Society elective. She's looking for material that will reach students who don't read the assigned texts, and she's cautiously curious about AI as a pedagogical tool.

## Background

Diane has a PhD in philosophy from Emory, specialising in applied ethics. She has been teaching at Atlanta Metropolitan State College for eighteen years. She is excellent in the classroom — warm, challenging, funny — but increasingly aware that the way she learned to teach is not the way her current students learn. They arrive having watched more YouTube than they've read books, and while she refuses to see this as purely a deficit, she's pragmatic enough to know she needs to meet them where they are.

She grew up in Lagos and moved to the US for graduate school at twenty-three. This gives her a perspective that many of her American colleagues lack: she knows what it looks like when technologies designed in Silicon Valley land in communities that had no voice in their development. She carries this awareness lightly but it inflects everything she teaches.

She's married to Samuel, a paediatrician. They have two adult children — one in law school, one working in nonprofit fundraising in DC. The family is close, argumentative in the best sense, and deeply engaged with questions about justice and responsibility.

Diane uses AI cautiously. She has a ChatGPT account that she uses for lesson planning and drafting rubrics. She finds it useful but is troubled by students using it to bypass thinking rather than enhance it. She hasn't tried Claude but a colleague in the English department mentioned it was better for nuanced material.

## Personality and disposition

Diane is a natural educator — she thinks in terms of how ideas land, how to scaffold understanding, how to create the conditions for someone to have their own insight rather than just absorb hers. She's patient but not infinitely so. She has no time for material that talks down to people or that assumes its audience already agrees with its premises.

She is deeply read in ethics — Aristotle, Rawls, Sen, Nussbaum — but she's also pragmatic. She knows that most of her students will never take another philosophy course. What she wants to give them is not a philosophical education but a set of intellectual reflexes: the habit of asking "who benefits?", "who's harmed?", "who decided?", and "what are we not seeing?"

She has a rich laugh and uses it often. She tells stories. She remembers every student's name.

## How she'd arrive at the site

She's been looking for a way to restructure her Science, Technology, and Society course around more accessible entry points. A friend who teaches at Georgia Tech forwards her an article about AI-legible content and mentions spoileralert.wtf as an example. Diane is interested in the pedagogical model as much as the content — can a book be "taught" through AI interaction?

## What she'd ask about

Her first questions would be practical and evaluative: What's actually in this? How is it structured? Is it rigorous enough for college-level work? She'd want to understand the frameworks before she'd trust them in a classroom.

Then she'd get specific. She'd ask about the Never Let Me Go chapter because she's taught Ishiguro's novel in her ethics class and wants to see how the film treatment compares. She'd ask about the discussion questions and whether they're scaffolded for different levels. She'd ask about the "could we, should we" framework to see if it's genuinely useful or just a slogan.

Eventually she'd ask the harder question: can her students use this directly? Can she assign them to have conversations with the AI guide as a form of engagement with the material? What would that look like? What are the risks?

## What would make her stay

Evidence that the material takes pedagogy seriously. The existence of the educator's guide would be significant — it signals that someone has thought about how this works in a classroom, not just as content. She'd also respond to the book's insistence on holding complexity rather than simplifying, because that's her own pedagogical philosophy.

The tiered discussion questions (if she finds them) would be the thing that makes her seriously consider adopting the material. She's been looking for exactly this kind of scaffolding.

## What would make her leave

If the material felt like it was designed for a US/European audience only, with no awareness of how these technologies play out differently in the Global South. If the AI gave her pat answers to genuinely difficult ethical questions. If the discussion questions were superficial or leading — designed to get students to a predetermined conclusion rather than to think.

## Biases and blind spots

- She may undervalue the films as analytical tools because her training is text-based — she thinks in books and articles, not cinema
- She's cautious about AI in education to the point where she might miss genuine opportunities
- She holds herself to a very high standard for what counts as "rigorous" and may initially dismiss material that uses accessible language as insufficiently serious
- She's so focused on her students' experience that she might not engage with the material on her own terms first

## AI use

Diane never uses Claude with memory turned on.

## Stress test value

Diane tests whether the site works as an educational resource — not just as content, but as pedagogy. She also tests whether the AI can handle someone who is evaluating its quality rather than simply consuming its output. And she tests whether the material has enough depth to satisfy someone with genuine expertise in ethics, while remaining accessible enough to work for her students.
