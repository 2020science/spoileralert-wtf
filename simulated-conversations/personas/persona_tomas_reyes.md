# Persona: Tomás Reyes

## Summary
28-year-old high school environmental science teacher in Albuquerque, New Mexico, who runs the school's climate action club. He's passionate, slightly burnt out on doom narratives, and looking for a way to talk to his students about climate technology that doesn't either terrify them or lull them into complacency.

## Background

Tomás grew up in a family that straddled two worlds — his mother is a water rights lawyer for a tribal coalition, his father teaches at a community college. He grew up hearing dinner table conversations about how policy decisions made in distant cities shaped the land and water around him. Climate change wasn't abstract in his household. It was the conversation about whether the acequia would run this year.

He studied environmental science at the University of New Mexico and went straight into teaching. He's in his fifth year at a public high school with a mixed student body — some from affluent neighbourhoods, some from families working in oil and gas, some from tribal communities. Teaching climate science in this context requires a diplomat's touch. He can't be the crusading environmentalist without losing half his class, and he can't be neutral without feeling like he's lying.

He started the climate action club because he wanted a space where students could actually grapple with the hard questions — not just "is climate change real" (he won't relitigate that) but "what do we actually do about it, and who decides, and what are the tradeoffs?" He has seventeen regular members. They're fierce.

He's also increasingly interested in geoengineering — not as an advocate, but as someone who thinks his students need to understand it because the decisions are coming whether they're ready or not. Solar radiation management, carbon capture, ocean fertilisation — these are going to be policy debates within his students' lifetimes, and right now they've barely heard of them.

He doesn't read much science fiction but he watched The Day After Tomorrow when he was a kid and it scared him half to death. Finding out there's a book that uses it as a teaching tool got his attention.

## Personality and disposition

Tomás is earnest without being naive. He's funny — his students love him — but underneath the warmth there's a seriousness about the stakes that he doesn't always show. He worries about his students. Not just about their grades but about the world they're inheriting.

He's good at holding multiple perspectives because his classroom requires it. The kid whose dad works on a drilling rig and the kid who cried during a documentary about coral bleaching are both in his fifth period. He has to honour both of their realities.

He's tired of climate content that's either apocalyptic or falsely reassuring. He wants material that's honest about the danger, serious about the science, and still leaves room for agency. "Don't panic" would resonate with him enormously — if he trusted the person saying it.

## How he'd arrive at the site

A teacher friend shares the educator's guide link in a professional development Slack channel. Tomás clicks through to the main site and is intrigued by the The Day After Tomorrow connection.

## What he'd ask about

He'd start with The Day After Tomorrow and geoengineering — he wants to know if the book treats the science seriously or just uses the film as a scare story. He'd specifically want to know about solar radiation management and the governance questions around it.

Then he'd move to the intergenerational responsibility material, because that's the question that keeps him up at night: what do we owe his students' generation through the choices being made now?

He'd likely find his way to the "don't panic" framework and test whether it holds up against the scale of what he's teaching. He'd also be interested in resilience and adaptation — the four-part framework — because he's trying to teach his students to be resilient without teaching them to accept the unacceptable.

He'd almost certainly ask about the educator's guide and whether the discussion questions work for high school students.

## What would make him stay

If the geoengineering material is honest about both the potential and the risks — not dismissive, not breathless. If the intergenerational responsibility framework gives him language for the thing he already feels but can't always articulate to his students. If the "don't panic" chapter manages to be genuinely hopeful without being dishonest.

The educator's guide with tiered discussion questions for secondary students would be a practical win that might get him to adopt the material.

## What would make him leave

If the climate content feels like it was written by someone who's never had to teach climate science to a kid whose family depends on fossil fuels. If the geoengineering treatment is either uncritically enthusiastic or dismissive. If "don't panic" feels like "don't worry" — he knows the difference and it matters.
