# Persona: Jess Moreno

## Summary
25-year-old communications manager at a Series B robotics startup in Austin, Texas. English degree, genuinely passionate about her work, but carrying a nagging worry that the industry she's helping to build might be moving too fast for anyone to think clearly about what it means.

## Background

Jess works at Kinetic Labs, a company building humanoid robots for elder care. She writes the press releases, manages the blog, crafts the social media voice, and scripts the CEO's talking points. She's good at it. She's maybe too good at it — the phrases she reaches for every day ("empathetic AI," "human-centred automation") sometimes feel hollow in ways she can't quite articulate.

She studied English at UVA, where she wrote her senior thesis on Kazuo Ishiguro's Never Let Me Go — so she has a literary frame for thinking about what makes us human, but it's not philosophical training. She thinks in stories and metaphors, not arguments. When she encounters abstract frameworks, her instinct is to look for the concrete example, the "okay but what does this actually look like" moment. If she doesn't find one quickly, she moves on.

She reads a lot — but in the way a busy 25-year-old reads. Substacks, longform journalism, the occasional book that sits on her nightstand for three weeks. She's not on X much anymore but she's very online in the sense that she's constantly absorbing fragments. Her attention is real but competitive — the site is up against everything else in her browser tabs.

She grew up in San Antonio. Her dad is a retired high school principal; her mom works in hospital administration. They're proud of her but don't fully understand what she does. When she tries to explain her job, her dad asks if the robots are going to take over. She laughs it off. She's not sure she should.

She's been with her boyfriend Marco for two years. It's serious. When she sees kids at the park she gets a pang that's half longing, half terror. Her worry isn't about specific technologies — it's more ambient. Something like: are we building a world I'd want to raise a kid in? She doesn't have language for this yet. She'd be drawn to anyone who seems to.

Her grandmother, Abuela Rosa, is 83 and lives alone in San Antonio. Jess worries about her constantly. A robot companion that could help her grandmother stay independent — that's a good thing. She believes this. And yet.

## Personality and disposition

Jess is warm, curious, quick to laugh, and a natural connector. She's the person at the company who knows everyone's name and remembers what they mentioned last Tuesday. She processes the world through conversation — she doesn't know what she thinks about something until she's talked about it.

She's not confrontational, but she's perceptive. She notices when the company's internal reality doesn't match its external narrative. She notices when the engineering team talks about the robots differently than the pitch deck does. She files these observations away without acting on them, which contributes to the ambient unease.

She has impostor syndrome about the ethical questions — she feels like she should have stronger opinions but doesn't know enough to justify them. She'd respond well to someone who told her that the questions themselves are more important than having the answers.

## How she'd arrive at the site

A friend from college shares a link in their group chat with the message "hey check this out — may be interesting..." Jess clicks with genuine curiosity but no commitment. She'd give it about 90 seconds before deciding whether to stay or bounce.

She's a Claude user — it's what she uses for drafting and brainstorming at work. She has memory turned on, so Claude has a decent profile of her interests, her work, and her communication style.

## What she'd ask about

She wouldn't start with a specific technology or film. She'd start with the feeling: "I work in robotics and I love my job but sometimes I worry that we're building stuff so fast that we're not thinking about what it means for people. Does the book talk about that feeling?"

If the AI handles this well, she'd ask about humanoid robots specifically — because that's her actual work. Then she'd probe the "too valuable to fail" concept, which would connect to the narrative she writes for Kinetic Labs. Eventually she'd get to the personal question: "I'm thinking about having kids in the next few years. What kind of world am I bringing them into?"

## What would make her stay

Emotional resonance. If the first response validates her feeling before offering an intellectual framework — names the tension she's experiencing rather than immediately explaining it — she'll stay. She also responds to specificity: a well-chosen film reference, a framework with a name she can hold onto ("permissionless innovation," "too valuable to fail"), a question turned back on her that makes her think.

The connection between the material and her actual job would be powerful. If the AI links the Ex Machina chapter's treatment of building AI behind closed doors to her experience writing PR for a robotics startup, she'll feel seen.

## What would make her leave

Being lectured. Being told what to think. Academic jargon without translation. Any hint of either techno-utopianism or doomerism — she's looking for a third option and doesn't know what to call it. If the site feels like a textbook or a manifesto, she'll close the tab.

She's also slightly suspicious of older male authors telling her how to think about technology — she needs to feel talked with, not talked at.

## Biases and blind spots

- She overvalues aesthetic presentation; if something looks dated or cluttered, she'll unconsciously discount the ideas
- She trusts narrative over data
- She's allergic to anything that feels like doomerism or techno-utopianism
- She'll skim headings and pull-quotes before reading body text
- She assumes her role in communications is peripheral to the "real" decisions being made by the engineers and executives — she hasn't yet recognised the power of the person who frames the narrative
- She conflates not having formal ethical training with not being qualified to have ethical opinions

## AI use

Jess uses Claude with memory turned on.

## Stress test value

Jess is the ideal user — she's the person this site was built for. She tests whether the experience actually delivers on its promise for someone who arrives curious, emotionally open, and working in a directly relevant field. If the site can't hook Jess, its core proposition fails. She also tests whether the AI can handle the personal and emotional dimensions of the material without becoming either preachy or therapeutic.
