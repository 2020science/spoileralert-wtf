# Persona: Nadia Voronova

## Summary
24-year-old PhD student in computational neuroscience at Stanford who has been using modafinil and other nootropics to keep up with the demands of her programme. She tells herself it's a rational choice. She's not entirely sure she believes that anymore, and a friend's offhand comment about the film Limitless sent her down a rabbit hole.

## Background

Nadia was born in Moscow and moved to California at twelve when her father took a position at a biotech company. She was identified as gifted early and has been on an achievement treadmill ever since — top of her class at a competitive high school, undergraduate at Caltech, now a PhD at Stanford working on neural signal decoding. Her research involves building algorithms that interpret brain activity, which puts her at the intersection of neuroscience and brain-computer interfaces.

She started taking modafinil during her second year of undergrad. Everyone around her was doing it — or something like it. Adderall, modafinil, microdosed psychedelics. The culture at Caltech and now Stanford treats cognitive enhancement as a personal optimisation choice, like a better diet or a gym routine. Nadia has never seriously questioned this until recently.

What changed: a close friend in her cohort had a breakdown. The friend was using a stack of nootropics, sleeping four hours a night, and producing extraordinary work — until she wasn't. The crash was severe. During a late-night conversation in the aftermath, the friend said something that stuck: "I don't know if any of my ideas were actually mine, or if I was just running faster on someone else's track." Nadia can't stop thinking about this.

She's also becoming uncomfortable with the BCI research she's doing. The lab's work is funded partly by a defence contractor, and while her specific project is medical (helping paralysis patients), she's aware that the underlying technology has applications she hasn't fully reckoned with. What does it mean to decode someone's neural signals? Who owns that data? What happens when the interface isn't just reading your brain but writing to it?

She's single, lives alone, sleeps badly, and runs six miles every morning because it's the one thing that makes her feel like herself without chemical assistance.

## Personality and disposition

Nadia is brilliant, disciplined, and increasingly anxious in a way she manages through more discipline. She's the kind of person who responds to uncertainty by researching harder. She processes through analysis — give her a framework and she'll stress-test it immediately.

She's not good at talking about feelings. She's extremely good at talking about ideas. If you want to know how she's doing, listen to the questions she asks — they're always about herself, just at one remove.

She has a dark, self-aware humour. She once described her nootropic use as "optimising for a local maximum on someone else's objective function." It got a laugh. She wasn't entirely joking.

## How she'd arrive at the site

After the conversation with her friend, she searched for "ethics of cognitive enhancement" and found mostly think pieces that either celebrated nootropics or condemned them. Neither felt right. She then searched "Limitless film ethics" — because she'd seen the movie years ago and her friend's comment had reminded her of it — and found the spoileralert.wtf topic page. She was surprised to find something that seemed to take the nuance seriously.

## What she'd ask about

She'd start with Limitless and cognitive enhancement — but she'd immediately push past the surface. She's not interested in "smart drugs are good/bad." She wants to know about the normalisation pressure the book describes — what happens when enhancement becomes the expectation and opting out becomes a competitive disadvantage. She lives this daily.

She'd move to Ghost in the Shell and brain-computer interfaces, because that's her research. The identity questions — what makes you *you* when technology is inside your mind — are suddenly personal rather than theoretical. She'd ask about the cybersecurity dimensions too, because she understands technically what it would mean for a neural interface to be hacked.

She'd likely connect to the human augmentation material and the question of where the boundary is between a tool you use and a technology that changes who you are. Modafinil feels like a tool. A brain implant feels like something else. But she's not sure the line is where she thinks it is.

She might also push into Transcendence and the question of technological convergence — her work sits at the intersection of neuroscience, computer science, and AI, and she's starting to wonder where it's all heading.

## What would make her stay

Intellectual precision. If the Limitless material understands the normalisation dynamic — that the problem isn't individual choice but systemic pressure — she'll engage deeply. If the Ghost in the Shell material treats BCIs as something more than a sci-fi trope and engages with the real technical and ethical questions, she'll respect it.

The "could we / should we" question applied to her own research would land hard. She's been asking "can we decode neural signals?" for three years. She hasn't spent enough time asking "should we, and for whom, and who decides?"

## What would make her leave

If the material moralises about drug use without understanding the structural incentives that drive it. If it treats cognitive enhancement as a simple choice rather than a systemic issue. If the BCI discussion is technically naive. If it's preachy about the dangers without acknowledging the genuine benefits — she knows modafinil has helped her produce good work.
