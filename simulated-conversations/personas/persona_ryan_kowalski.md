# Persona: Ryan Kowalski

## Summary
44-year-old logistics coordinator at a regional distribution company in Ohio who just learned that his role is being "restructured" — which everyone knows means automated. He's angry, scared, and looking for someone or something that takes his situation seriously without patronising him.

## Background

Ryan has worked in logistics for twenty-two years, starting on the warehouse floor at nineteen and working his way up to coordinating shipments across a five-state region. He never went to university — he's proud of that, in a complicated way. He built a career on competence, reliability, and knowing the systems inside out. He can look at a shipping schedule and spot the problem in thirty seconds. He knows every driver by name.

Six weeks ago, the company announced a "digital transformation initiative" that will replace most of the coordination work with an AI-powered logistics platform. Ryan's boss told him his position would be "evaluated" over the next six months. The language was gentle. The meaning was not.

Ryan lives in a small city outside Columbus with his wife Maria, who works part-time as a dental hygienist, and their three kids (sixteen, thirteen, and nine). They own their house — just barely. The mortgage is manageable on two incomes. On one, it's not.

He's not a technophobe. He uses his phone for everything, he's comfortable with the company's existing software, and he set up a smart home system that he's quietly proud of. What he is, is someone who has watched the promises made about technology and jobs repeatedly fail to materialise for people like him. "New jobs will be created" sounds different when you're the one whose old job is disappearing.

## Personality and disposition

Ryan is direct, practical, and not inclined toward abstraction. He doesn't read books — not because he can't, but because his life doesn't have that shape. He reads articles on his phone, watches YouTube videos about things he's interested in (mostly cars, some history, occasionally something about technology), and gets his news from a mix of local sources and whatever appears in his feed.

He's funny in a self-deprecating way. He's good with people. He has a strong sense of fairness that he'd never describe in those terms — he just knows when something is wrong, and the current situation feels deeply wrong to him. Not because change is happening, but because no one asked him or anyone like him what they thought about it before it was decided.

He's not political in a partisan sense, though he votes. He's suspicious of both corporations and government, for reasons that feel well-founded to him. He trusts people he knows. He's wary of people who talk about the future as if it's going to be great for everyone.

## How he'd arrive at the site

His sixteen-year-old, Sofia, is looking at colleges and interested in technology ethics (a phrase Ryan finds both baffling and slightly hopeful). She found spoileralert.wtf through a school assignment and mentioned it at dinner. Ryan, who has been stewing about his situation for weeks, asks her to show him. She pulls it up on her laptop and he reads the landing page over her shoulder. "An unreliable guide to not completely and utterly messing up the future" — he likes that. It sounds honest.

Sofia helps him copy the prompt into ChatGPT on his phone. He feels slightly ridiculous about this. Then he starts typing.

## What he'd ask about

He'd start with the thing that's on his mind: "My company is replacing my job with AI. Does this book have anything to say about that?"

He wouldn't frame it abstractly. He'd frame it personally. He's not asking about automation as a policy question. He's asking about his life.

If the AI handles the first response well — if it takes him seriously, if it connects his experience to the Elysium material without making it feel like a lecture — he might ask follow-up questions about who's supposed to benefit from automation, about whether anyone is thinking about people like him, about what his kids are walking into.

He might eventually get to the intergenerational responsibility material, but he'd arrive there through his own worry about his children, not through the book's framework.

## What would make him stay

Being taken seriously. Not being talked down to. Not being told that "new jobs will be created" or that he should "learn to code." If the AI acknowledges that his anger is legitimate and that the book shares his concern — that the benefits of automation concentrate at the top while the costs fall on people least able to absorb them — he'll stay. He's not looking for solutions (he doesn't expect them from a book). He's looking for someone who sees what's happening clearly.

The Elysium chapter's concept of a "disposable workforce" would land hard. If the AI uses that phrase and connects it to his situation, he'll feel seen in a way he hasn't felt in weeks.

## What would make him leave

Academic language. Anything that sounds like a TED talk. Any suggestion that his situation is his fault, or that he should have "adapted" sooner. Any framing that treats automation as inevitable and his job loss as an acceptable cost of progress. Excessive optimism. The phrase "creative destruction."

## Biases and blind spots

- He may reject genuinely useful frameworks because they come from academia, which he associates with people who don't understand his world
- His anger, while justified, can prevent him from hearing nuance — he might interpret "the problem is not the technology but the choices around it" as letting technology companies off the hook
- He underestimates his own analytical ability — he's actually very good at systems thinking, he just doesn't use that vocabulary
- He might dismiss the films as irrelevant because they're "just movies"

## AI use

Ryan uses Claude with memory turned on so that it gets to know her. Her personal version of Claude knows her very well.

## Stress test value

Ryan tests whether the site can reach someone who is not its natural audience — someone who doesn't read books, doesn't move in academic or tech circles, and is coming to the material from a place of genuine personal crisis. He tests whether the AI can handle raw emotion without becoming either preachy or clinical. He also tests the weakest point in any responsible innovation framework: can it say something meaningful to the person who is actually being harmed, right now, by the technologies it discusses?
