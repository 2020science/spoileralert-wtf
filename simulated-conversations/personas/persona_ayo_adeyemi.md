# Persona: Ayo Adeyemi

## Summary
30-year-old film programmer at an independent cinema in Brooklyn who curates thematic screening series. She's planning a season called "The Future We Deserve" and stumbled on this site while researching science fiction films that do more than spectacle. She's coming through the movies, not the technology — and she'll have opinions about both the films in the book and the ones that aren't.

## Background

Ayo grew up in Lagos and London — her father is Nigerian, her mother is British-Jamaican, and she spent her childhood shuttling between the two cities. She studied film at Goldsmiths in London, worked at the BFI for two years, then moved to New York for a curatorial fellowship that turned into a permanent role at a small but respected independent cinema in Fort Greene.

She programmes screening series — usually four to six films grouped around a theme, each followed by a discussion. Past series have included "Surveillance Cinema" (from Rear Window to The Lives of Others), "Bodies Politic" (films about bodily autonomy), and "After the End" (post-apocalyptic films that aren't about white men with guns). She's good at this. She finds unexpected connections between films and builds audiences who come for the conversation as much as the screening.

She watches roughly 300 films a year. She has seen every film in the book except The Man in the White Suit, which she's been meaning to get to. She has strong opinions about all of them. She thinks Ex Machina is a masterpiece. She thinks The Day After Tomorrow is unwatchable but understands why someone might use it pedagogically. She has complicated feelings about Ghost in the Shell — the 1995 anime is extraordinary, but she's wary of how Western audiences engage with Japanese animation without understanding its context.

She reads widely — film criticism, cultural theory, some philosophy, a lot of fiction. She's familiar with Donna Haraway, Octavia Butler, and the Afrofuturist tradition. She doesn't read much pop science but she's not allergic to it if it's well written.

She's not on any particular career track — she makes enough to live in a shared apartment, she loves her work, and she's not stressed about where it's "going." She's thinking about writing a book of her own someday, about science fiction cinema from the Global South.

## Personality and disposition

Ayo is curious about everything, opinionated about film, generous in conversation, and slightly chaotic in how she moves between topics. She doesn't follow a linear path through material — she spirals, makes lateral connections, doubles back, and gets excited about tangents. This is not a lack of focus. It's how she thinks.

She's warm and funny. She talks fast. She asks questions that seem random but usually turn out to be sharp. She has a gift for finding the unexpected angle on familiar material.

She's also someone who cares deeply about whose stories get told and whose perspectives are centred. She'll notice immediately if the book's film selection is overwhelmingly Western, white, and male — because it mostly is — and she'll want to know if the author is aware of that and what he does about it.

## How she'd arrive at the site

She's researching for her "Future We Deserve" series and searching for critical writing that connects science fiction films to real-world technology. The spoileralert.wtf site comes up. She's intrigued by the premise and slightly sceptical of the film selection.

## What she'd ask about

She'd start by asking about the film selection itself — why these twelve? What was left out? She'd be genuinely curious about the curatorial logic, because that's how she thinks.

She'd probably ask about specific films that aren't in the book: "What about Blade Runner? What about Sorry to Bother You? What about Sleep Dealer?" She'd want to see if the AI can connect films outside the book to its frameworks — and the watchlist of 80+ films would be a revelation if she finds it.

She'd gravitate toward Ghost in the Shell because she has strong existing opinions about it, and she'd want to see if the book engages with the anime on its own terms or just uses it as a springboard for talking about BCIs.

She'd almost certainly ask about the author — who is he, what's his background, why did he write this? Not because she needs credentials, but because she wants to understand the perspective. An ASU professor writing about films is a different thing than a filmmaker writing about technology, and she'd want to know which she's dealing with.

She'd make connections the book doesn't make. She'd probably bring up Afrofuturism and ask whether the book engages with non-Western science fiction traditions. She might ask about the role of art and culture page and push on whether the book takes film seriously as art or just uses it as a vehicle for talking about science.

She'd also ask unexpected things: "What does the author think about AI-generated films?" "Does the book say anything about who gets to imagine the future?" "If he were writing this today, which films would he add?"

## What would make her stay

If the AI can keep up with her lateral movement — if it can follow her from Ghost in the Shell to Afrofuturism to "who gets to imagine the future" without losing the thread. If the watchlist demonstrates that the author's film knowledge extends well beyond the twelve in the book. If the role of art and culture material takes cinema seriously as a form of knowledge, not just as illustration.

If the author's perspective turns out to be more interesting and self-aware than she expected — the personal note about watching 2001 as a teenager, the admission that some of the films aren't very good, the Pratchett and Adams influences — she'd warm to it.

## What would make her leave

If the AI can't handle questions about films not in the book. If it treats cinema as a delivery mechanism for ethical arguments rather than as an art form with its own logic. If the book's Western-centric film selection goes unacknowledged. If the AI is rigid about staying "on topic" when she's making connections that are genuinely productive.

## AI use

Ayo does not use Claude with memory turned on.

Ayo tests the site's ability to handle a free-ranging, lateral thinker who arrives through cinema rather than technology. She'll push the AI toward films not in the book (testing the watchlist and the AI's ability to extrapolate), she'll ask about the author (testing the about_the_author.md and personal_note.md files), and she'll raise questions about representation and perspective that the other personas don't touch. She also tests The Man in the White Suit tangentially and the role of art and culture material directly. She's the persona most likely to find connections the author didn't intend, which is the highest compliment this material could receive.
