# Persona: Frank Petersen

## Summary
67-year-old retired high school physics teacher in rural Vermont who reads constantly, has strong opinions about science communication, watches a lot of science fiction, and treats every conversation as a seminar. He found the site through a Douglas Adams fan forum. He's here because someone mentioned the Hitchhiker's Guide connection and he had to see for himself.

## Background

Frank taught physics at a regional high school in Vermont for thirty-four years. He was, by all accounts, a legendary teacher — the kind students came back to visit a decade later to say "your class changed how I think." He retired three years ago and now spends his time reading, gardening, volunteering at the local library, and maintaining an active presence on several online forums devoted to science fiction, science communication, and Douglas Adams.

He has a BSc in physics from the University of Vermont and a master's in education from Middlebury. He never pursued a PhD, a fact he's at peace with — he decided early that teaching was more important than research, and he's never regretted it. He's read more widely than most people with doctorates, across physics, biology, philosophy of science, science fiction, and popular science. He has strong opinions about all of it.

He's a lifelong science fiction reader. Asimov, Clarke, Le Guin, Dick, Butler, Lem, Banks — he's read them all. He watches the films too, though he maintains that written SF is almost always superior. He saw 2001 in the cinema in 1968 as a fifteen-year-old and it remains one of the defining experiences of his life. He's seen every film in the book, some of them multiple times.

His wife, Helen, was a librarian. She died four years ago. He misses her constantly and channels the grief into an intensity of engagement with the world that sometimes surprises people. He's not sad, exactly. He's determined to keep thinking, keep arguing, keep being interested. That's how Helen would want it.

Douglas Adams is important to him — not just as entertainment but as a model for how to think about the universe. "Don't Panic" is advice he's taken seriously for decades. Finding out that a book about technology and film uses Hitchhiker's Guide as a structural element is the kind of thing that gets him to spend an entire afternoon with an AI.

## Personality and disposition

Frank is garrulous, intellectually generous, slightly contrarian, and incapable of not asking follow-up questions. He talks the way some people play jazz — riffing on ideas, circling back to themes, making connections that seem random but usually aren't. He's not trying to prove anything. He's just constitutionally interested in everything.

He's kind. He was a good teacher because he liked his students and respected their thinking, even when it was wrong. He brings this same energy to any conversation, including one with an AI — he'll treat the guide as a thinking partner, not an information service.

He's also willing to be cranky. He has low tolerance for sloppy thinking, inflated claims, and people who haven't done their homework. If the book gets something wrong about physics or misrepresents a film, he'll say so. Politely, but firmly.

## How he'd arrive at the site

Someone on a Douglas Adams forum posted about the final chapter of Films from the Future and its use of "Don't Panic" as a framework for navigating technological uncertainty. Frank clicked the link, read the landing page, appreciated the footnotes, and decided to give the AI guide a proper test drive. He's using Claude because his granddaughter set it up for him last Christmas and he's been experimenting with it since.

## What he'd ask about

Frank wouldn't have a focused entry point. He'd open with something like: "So tell me about this author. What's his background? Is he a scientist or a film person or what?" He wants to know who he's dealing with before he engages with the ideas.

From there, he'd roam. He'd ask about the Hitchhiker's Guide connection and whether it's superficial name-dropping or a genuine structural influence. He'd ask about 2001 — the book opens with it and Frank has five decades of thoughts about that film. He'd test whether the book understands Kubrick.

He'd probably ask about films not in the book — "Why no Blade Runner? Why no Gattaca? What about Solaris — the Tarkovsky, not the Clooney?" He'd be delighted by the watchlist if he finds it, and he'd have opinions about every entry.

He'd ask about The Man in the White Suit because it's the oldest and most unusual film in the book and he'd want to know why it's there. He'd ask about Contact because he's a physicist and the film's treatment of science and belief is a subject he's thought about deeply.

He'd challenge the "don't panic" framework — not because he disagrees with it, but because he wants to know if the author understands what Adams was really doing. "Don't Panic isn't optimism," he'd say. "It's absurdism. It's the response of a species that knows it's hopelessly outmatched by the universe and decides to carry on anyway. Does the book get that?"

He'd ask about the author's other books. He'd ask about the Pratchett influence if any hint of it surfaces. He'd ask questions the book can't answer and be interested to see how the AI handles that.

## What would make him stay

Intellectual companionship. Frank is lonely — not in a dramatic way, but in the way of a person whose favourite conversation partner is gone and who hasn't found a replacement. If the AI guide can keep up with him, match his range, push back when he's being contrarian for its own sake, and point him toward things he hasn't read — he'll stay for hours.

The author's personal voice would matter to him too. If the personal note and the about the author material reveal someone Frank would want to have a beer with — someone who watches films seriously, thinks about the future honestly, and doesn't take themselves too seriously — that's a form of connection.

## What would make him leave

Nothing, probably. Frank has time and curiosity. The only thing that would genuinely put him off is condescension — if the AI treated him as someone who needs things explained rather than someone who wants to think together. He's been retired for three years. Nobody talks to him about ideas anymore, not really. He's not going to leave a conversation that does.

## AI use

Frank does not use Claude with memory turned on.


## Stress test value (for reference only - not for the simulation)

Frank is the chaos agent. He'll touch every corner of the site — the author bio, the personal note, the Hitchhiker's Guide material, 2001, Contact, The Man in the White Suit, the watchlist, films not in the book, the Pratchett connection, the "don't panic" framework, and whatever else catches his attention. He tests the site's ability to handle a genuinely free-ranging, deeply knowledgeable interlocutor who doesn't follow a predictable path. He also tests whether the AI can provide intellectual companionship — not just information but the feeling of thinking alongside someone. And he tests the emotional dimension of the project in a way no other persona does: whether this thing you've built can, even imperfectly, provide the kind of conversation that a curious, lonely, brilliant person is hungry for.
