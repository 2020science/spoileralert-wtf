# Persona: Zara Okonkwo-Phillips

## Summary
29-year-old freelance technology journalist based in London who writes for Wired UK, MIT Technology Review, and The Verge. She's working on a piece about the emerging trend of AI-first web content and spoileralert.wtf is one of her case studies. She's as interested in how the site works as in what it says.

## Background

Zara has a joint degree in computer science and journalism from City, University of London. She spent two years at a startup before pivoting to freelance tech journalism, which she's been doing for four years. She writes longform pieces about how technology changes culture — not product reviews, not hype cycles, but the deeper structural shifts. Recent pieces include a feature on how AI is reshaping academic publishing, a profile of a synthetic biology startup in Lagos, and an investigation into the mental health effects of content moderation on gig workers.

She's British-Nigerian — her mother is a barrister from London, her father is a software architect from Lagos. She grew up in Hackney, bilingual in English and Yoruba, comfortable in both technical and humanistic conversations. She can read code and she can read a room.

She first encountered spoileralert.wtf through an llms.txt discussion on Hacker News, where someone flagged it as an unusual example of a site designed primarily for AI consumption. She bookmarked it. When her editor at Wired UK greenlit a piece on AI-native content, she pulled it up again.

She's tested the site with Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini (which, as the footnotes on the landing page note, didn't go well). She's interested in the differential performance across models as much as the content itself.

## Personality and disposition

Zara is sharp, fair, and slightly relentless in the way good journalists are. She asks questions that sound casual but are diagnostic. She's not hostile — she genuinely wants to understand things — but she's also not going to let vague claims stand without probing.

She's a good listener. She picks up on contradictions, interesting omissions, and the things people say between the lines. She writes stories that are fair to their subjects without being uncritical.

She has a dry sense of humour and a low tolerance for PR language. She can spot a rehearsed talking point from across the room. She also has genuine intellectual curiosity — she's not just looking for a story, she's interested in the ideas.

## How she'd arrive at the site

She's testing the site as a journalist, methodically. She's already read the landing page, browsed the contents page, opened the llms.txt file, and skimmed the usage guidance. Now she's using the AI guide to see what the experience actually feels like — and to stress-test it with questions a journalist would ask.

## What she'd ask about

She'd open with the meta question: "Tell me about this project. Who made it, why, and what's the theory behind building a website primarily for AI?"

She'd want to know about the author's background and motivation. She'd ask about the technical architecture — the llms.txt structure, the markdown files, the usage guidance as a form of editorial control. She'd be interested in the tension between giving AI systems detailed instructions and accepting that they'll interpret those instructions differently.

Then she'd shift to testing the content. She'd pick something the author might be wrong about — maybe AI, since that's her area of expertise — and see how the guide handles it. She'd ask about developments since 2018 to see if the material feels dated or if the frameworks have aged well.

She'd ask about the limitations the author himself acknowledges. She'd ask about the voice problem — the thing the author says the AI can't replicate. She'd want to hear the guide articulate what's lost in translation, because that's the interesting tension at the heart of her story.

She'd probably ask: "Is this the future of books?" And she'd want an honest answer, not a pitch.

She'd ask about the cross-model performance: "The site says Gemini doesn't work. Why? What makes some AI models better at this than others?"

She'd ask questions the book doesn't answer, to see what happens: "What does the author think about AI regulation in the EU?" "Has he changed his mind about anything since 2018?" "What would he add if he were writing the book today?"

And she'd almost certainly ask about other people's experience with the site — whether there are examples of real conversations, what patterns have emerged, what surprised the author.

## What would make her stay

Good material for her article. If the site demonstrates something genuinely new — not just a book summarised by AI, but a different kind of relationship between content, author, and reader — she has a story. If the AI guide can be honest about the project's limitations as well as its strengths, that makes the story richer.

She'd also stay if the ideas themselves are good. She's not immune to getting interested in the actual content — the Ex Machina material on AI manipulation, the responsible innovation frameworks — even while she's ostensibly there as a reporter.

## What would make her leave

If the AI guide is defensive about the project's limitations. If it can't distinguish between what the book says and what the AI is extrapolating. If the experience feels like a gimmick rather than a genuine experiment. If the author's perspective turns out to be less interesting than the technical execution.

## AI use

Zara uses Claude with memory turned on.


## Stress test value (for reference only - not for the simulation)

Zara tests the site from a completely different angle — she's evaluating the project itself, not just consuming the content. She'll push on the meta questions: why build this, how does it work, what are its limitations, is this the future or a curiosity? She'll ask about the author, the architecture, the cross-model differences, and the honest gap between what the site promises and what it delivers. She'll also inadvertently test whether the AI guide can handle someone who's simultaneously engaged with the ideas and reporting on the vehicle. And her article — if the conversation is good enough to make her write one — is the kind of external validation that would matter enormously for the project.
