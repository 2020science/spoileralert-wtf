# Persona: Priya Ramachandran

## Summary
31-year-old postdoctoral researcher in biosecurity at Johns Hopkins. She works on pandemic preparedness policy and has strong, complicated feelings about gain-of-function research. She's seen the Inferno chapter referenced in an academic newsletter and wants to see if a pop-science treatment has anything to offer someone who works in the field.

## Background

Priya has a PhD in molecular biology from MIT and pivoted into biosecurity policy during the COVID-19 pandemic, when she realised that the governance questions interested her more than the bench work. She now spends her days writing policy briefs about dual-use research oversight, attending workshops on laboratory biosafety, and trying to explain to non-scientists why gain-of-function research is simultaneously one of the most important and most dangerous things happening in biology.

She was born in Chennai and moved to the US at eighteen for university. Her parents are both doctors — her father a cardiologist, her mother a paediatrician — and the family culture treats science as a vocation, not just a career. She carries an awareness that biosecurity failures don't hit every country equally, and that the governance frameworks she works on are overwhelmingly designed by and for wealthy nations.

She lives in Baltimore with her partner Kai, who is a documentary filmmaker. They argue productively about the gap between how scientists talk about risk and how the public experiences it. These arguments have made her a better communicator.

She's read widely — not just in her field but in science fiction. She's seen the Inferno film and found it ridiculous but was intrigued when she heard the book treatment was more nuanced. She's also a fan of Contact and has strong opinions about the relationship between science and belief.

## Personality and disposition

Priya is precise, direct, and slightly impatient with material that oversimplifies her field. She knows the gain-of-function debate from the inside and has sat through too many panels where people with strong opinions had weak knowledge. She's not looking for an introduction to the topic — she's looking for a framework that adds something to what she already knows.

That said, she's genuinely curious and capable of being surprised. If the book's treatment of Inferno goes beyond the usual "dual-use dilemma" framing, she'll engage deeply. She's particularly interested in the concept of "immoral logic" — the idea that someone can follow a chain of reasoning that is internally consistent but morally catastrophic.

She communicates clearly, asks sharp questions, and will push back on anything she considers imprecise. She's not hostile — she's rigorous.

## How she'd arrive at the site

A colleague in the biosecurity community shared a link to the Inferno topic page with the note "surprisingly good for a movie book." Priya follows the trail to the main site.

## What she'd ask about

She'd go straight for the Inferno material — gain-of-function research, synthetic biology, biosecurity, the "immoral logic" framework. She'd test whether the book understands the actual science and governance landscape.

If that holds up, she'd likely move to Contact — the science and belief chapter — because the relationship between evidence, certainty, and conviction is something she grapples with in her own field. Scientists who are certain they're right about gain-of-function benefits use the same rhetorical structures as Zobrist in Inferno.

She might also ask about the "honest broker" framework and how it applies to scientists who claim to be providing neutral advice while actually advocating for a position.

## What would make her stay

Technical accuracy and intellectual rigour. If the book's treatment of gain-of-function research is grounded in real science — not Hollywood virology — she'll take the ethical frameworks seriously. The "immoral logic" concept has genuine utility in her field and she'd want to explore it.

## What would make her leave

Scientific errors. Oversimplification of the dual-use dilemma. Any suggestion that gain-of-function research should simply be banned (she thinks it's more complicated than that). Treating biosecurity as a thought experiment rather than a real governance challenge with real stakes.
