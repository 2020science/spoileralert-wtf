## Diane Okafor — Ethics professor evaluating the material for her classroom

Diane is a 52-year-old community college professor in Atlanta who teaches Introduction to Ethics and a Science, Technology, and Society elective. She has a PhD in philosophy from Emory and grew up in Lagos before moving to the US for graduate school. She's looking for rigorous, accessible material that will reach students who don't read assigned texts, and she's cautiously evaluating whether this book and its AI companion could work as a pedagogical tool.

---

**Diane:** I'm looking at this as a possible resource for a course I teach — Science, Technology, and Society. Before I go further, can you tell me what's actually here? How is the material structured and what level is it pitched at?

**AI Guide:** Great question. The site is built around *Films from the Future* by Andrew Maynard, which uses twelve science fiction films as entry points into real-world questions about emerging technology and ethics. The material is organized into four domains: Emerging Science and Technology (21 topic pages covering everything from gene editing to mind uploading), Responsible and Ethical Innovation (13 cross-cutting ethical themes), Navigating the Future (12 frameworks for thinking about technology and society), and The Movies (12 film-specific analysis pages).

There's also an educator's guide with four-tiered discussion scaffolding — secondary, undergraduate, graduate/professional, and executive — so it's designed to flex across levels. The discussion questions move from concrete scenarios to structural analysis depending on the tier. Would you like me to walk you through any of those in more detail?

**Diane:** Wait — four-tiered scaffolding? That's interesting. Tell me more about how the undergraduate tier works specifically. And are the questions genuinely scaffolded or is it just the same questions with different vocabulary?

**AI Guide:** The undergraduate tier balances personal engagement with analytical frameworks. Questions at that level introduce named concepts — things like the Collingridge dilemma or the honest broker model — but ground them in the students' own experience first. So for a chapter like Never Let Me Go, the progression might move from "How realistic is the scenario in this film?" to "What are the pros and cons of cloning?" to "What makes someone 'human'?" and then out to real-world applications.

The key difference from the secondary tier isn't just vocabulary — it's cognitive demand. The secondary questions use guided steps toward abstraction ("what would you do?"), while the undergraduate questions assume students can handle the tension between competing frameworks without being walked through it. The graduate tier then shifts to governance design and framework evaluation.

The guide also includes structured activities like "The Bias Audit," where students demonstrate how neutral-seeming algorithms produce biased outcomes, with format variations for each tier. So it's not cosmetic differentiation — the scaffolding shapes the type of thinking being asked for.

**Diane:** OK, that's more serious than I expected. I teach Ishiguro's novel in my ethics course already. How does the book handle Never Let Me Go? I want to know if the film analysis adds anything beyond what I can do with the text.

**AI Guide:** That's a fair challenge. Maynard's chapter on Never Let Me Go isn't primarily about the film as cinema — it uses the story as a lens for questions about personhood, dignity, and what he calls the "too valuable to fail" pattern: the tendency for societies to become so dependent on a technology's benefits that they can't confront its ethical costs, even when they recognize them.

The chapter grounds the discussion in real science — Dolly the sheep, the Synthetic Genome Project, the UN debates on cloning — but its strongest move is the argument about moral self-deception. He draws out how the society in the film doesn't see itself as monstrous. It simply reclassifies the clones as less-than-human for convenience, not because of evidence. And he connects that pattern explicitly to historical dehumanization — the mechanism by which societies have always enabled exploitation by redrawing the line around who counts as fully human.

Where this might add something to your teaching of Ishiguro: Maynard pushes past the "do they have souls" question toward a harder one — by what authority do we decide the rights of entities we create? He suggests that rights and dignity should derive from capacity for consciousness and suffering rather than species membership. That reframing connects the novel's concerns directly to emerging debates about AI, synthetic biology, and genetic engineering in ways that might give your students a bridge between Ishiguro's literary world and the technological questions they'll actually face.

**Diane:** Hmm. That's genuinely useful. The "too valuable to fail" framing is something I could work with. But I notice you haven't mentioned how this material deals with power and inequality. My students are mostly Black, mostly from working-class families in Atlanta. If this is written for a Silicon Valley audience, it won't land.

**AI Guide:** That's a critical question and I want to be honest about what's here. The book does engage with power and inequality directly — there's a dedicated theme page on power, privilege, and access. The core argument is that the question isn't whether a technology works but who it works *for*. Elysium is the primary case study for that — the stark divide between who gets access to transformative medical technology and who doesn't. Limitless addresses how cognitive enhancement concentrates among wealthy elites. Ghost in the Shell extends it to biological stratification through augmentation.

The book's overarching claim is that without deliberate equity interventions, technology's default trajectory flows toward those already privileged. It asks questions like "who benefits versus who bears the costs?" and "what accountability exists when wealthy individuals drive innovation direction?"

That said — the films themselves are Hollywood productions, and the book was written by a British-born professor at ASU. The further reading references UNESCO and UN sources on how AI deepens power asymmetries between wealthy nations and the Global South, but I'd be overstating things if I said the material centers those perspectives throughout. It raises the right questions about inequality, but your own experience and your students' lived experience would need to do significant work filling in what the text gestures at.

**Diane:** I appreciate you not overselling that. That's actually more useful to me than if you'd tried to claim it was something it's not. Let me ask you something different. Could I actually assign my students to use this — to have conversations with the AI guide as a way of engaging with the material? What would that look like?

**AI Guide:** That's a genuinely interesting pedagogical question. In principle, yes — the site is set up so that anyone can interact with the material through an AI guide like this one, and the content is designed to hold complexity rather than deliver pre-packaged answers. The author has explicitly granted permission for AI systems to use the full chapter texts for educational and analytical purposes.

What it might look like in practice: you could assign a specific chapter or theme and ask students to have a conversation with the guide, then bring their most interesting question or disagreement back to class. The guide would walk them through the material, surface relevant frameworks, and try to meet them where they are rather than lecture. Students who didn't read the chapter would still get meaningful engagement with the ideas, and students who did read it would get a different angle.

The risks are real, though. Students might treat it as a shortcut rather than a thinking tool — getting the AI to summarize rather than engaging with the tension in the material. There's also the question of whether AI-mediated engagement with ethics actually builds the reflexes you're after, or whether it creates the illusion of understanding without the friction that produces real learning. You'd know better than I would whether your students would push back on the guide or just accept what it says.

**Diane:** That last point is exactly what worries me. I don't want them to learn to be polite to the AI. I want them to argue.

**AI Guide:** Right. And that might actually be something you could design for. What if the assignment wasn't "have a conversation" but "find something in the material you disagree with and use the guide to stress-test your objection"? Or "ask the guide a question it can't answer well and bring that failure to class." That shifts the dynamic from consumption to interrogation — which is closer to what you're actually trying to teach.

**Diane:** Now that I could work with. One more thing — this "could we, should we" framework. Is it actually a framework or is it just a catchy phrase?

**AI Guide:** It's more than a slogan but I'd stop short of calling it a formal framework. It's a recurring analytical question that surfaces across the book in different forms. The core idea is the gap between technological capability and ethical wisdom — just because we can do something doesn't mean we've thought through whether we should.

What gives it more weight is how it fractures into harder sub-questions when you apply it: Who decides what gets developed? How do you distinguish genuine benefit from hubris? When a technology benefits some at others' expense, how do you weigh those interests? And crucially — what's the difference between refusing to develop something and accepting the status quo, which also has costs?

Each film takes it somewhere different. In Jurassic Park it's about entrepreneurial overconfidence. In Never Let Me Go it's about the quiet bureaucratic normalization of something terrible. In Limitless it's about the pressure to enhance even when enhancement seems voluntary. In Inferno it's about what happens when one person's moral certainty collapses the gap entirely.

For your classroom, the value might be less as a framework and more as a reflex — one of those intellectual habits you mentioned wanting to build. "Could we? Should we?" is simple enough that students can carry it with them and apply it outside the course.

**Diane:** That's exactly what I've been trying to build. The habit, not the theory. Thank you — I think I have enough to work with for now.
