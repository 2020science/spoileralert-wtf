## Frank Petersen — Retired physics teacher who has been waiting thirty-four years for someone to take Douglas Adams as seriously as he does

Frank is a 67-year-old retired high school physics teacher in Vermont who reads voraciously, watches science fiction with the attention most people reserve for scripture, and treats every conversation as an opportunity to think out loud. He found the site through a Douglas Adams forum and arrived with five decades of opinions about 2001, an instinctive distrust of sloppy thinking, and the particular hunger of a brilliant person whose best conversation partner is gone.

**Frank:** So before I go any further — tell me about this author. What's his background? Is he a scientist or a film person or what?

**AI Guide:** Andrew Maynard is a scientist first. He has a PhD in physics from Cambridge, started in particle physics and aerosol science, then moved into nanotechnology risk and eventually into the broader ethics of emerging technology. He's a professor at Arizona State University now, running something called the Risk Innovation Lab.

But he's not purely academic — he cites Douglas Adams and Terry Pratchett as stylistic influences, he discovered science fiction watching 2001 at sixteen, and he's explicit that the book is meant to be a conversation, not a lecture. He also has a YouTube channel on risk science and has written two other books since this one.

Does that help frame things, or would you like more detail on any of that?

**Frank:** Wait. Pratchett AND Adams? That's an unusual pairing for a physicist. Most scientists who write for a general audience model themselves on Sagan or Feynman. Pratchett and Adams are both fundamentally satirists — they use comedy to expose the absurdity of systems, not to explain them. That's a very different project from science communication.

Ok but more importantly. The Hitchhiker's Guide connection. Is it real or is it just a cute reference? Because I've seen people slap "Don't Panic" on everything from self-help books to crypto newsletters and it drives me slightly insane.

**AI Guide:** It's structural, not decorative. The final chapter uses *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* as its organizing framework — the book is explicitly modeled as an incomplete guide, the way Adams' fictional Guide covers only what a traveler actually needs rather than the whole galaxy. The idea is that you can't map every emerging technology, but you can learn how to think about any of them.

And the "Don't Panic" part is specifically about rejecting both blind techno-optimism and dystopian fatalism. The argument is that panic leads to disengagement — people who believe the technological future is inevitably dark stop participating in the democratic processes that actually shape it.

The first chapter opens with 2001 and the last closes with Hitchhiker's Guide. They're bookends.

**Frank:** That's more promising than I expected. Though I'd push back on one thing. "Don't Panic" in Adams isn't really about balanced engagement or clear-headed thinking. It's absurdism. It's the response of a species that knows it's hopelessly outmatched by the universe and decides to carry on anyway. The whole joke is that the advice is printed on the cover of a book that's wildly inaccurate and mostly useless. Does the author get that? Or does he sanitize it into something more... professionally palatable?

**AI Guide:** That's a sharp distinction and I think you've put your finger on a real tension. The book uses "Don't Panic" earnestly — as a genuine framework for responsible engagement with technology. The author's version means: acknowledge the dangers, don't be paralyzed by them, recognize your agency. It's sincere advice.

So honestly? I think the answer is that he takes the spirit of Adams seriously — the idea that the universe is vastly more complicated than we can comprehend and we should be humble about that — but he does domesticate the absurdism into something more actionable. He's using Adams as a starting point for civic responsibility, which Adams himself would probably have found very funny.

Whether that's a strength or a weakness probably depends on what you think the book is trying to do.

**Frank:** Ha. Adams would have found it hilarious. And then he would have written a footnote explaining that the author had entirely missed the point, which would itself have been funnier than the original point. But fine. I'll give the man credit for at least using the structure seriously rather than just name-dropping.

Now. 2001. You said the author first watched it at sixteen. When was this?

**AI Guide:** January 1st, 1982. He watched it on a small black-and-white television in his parents' living room wearing headphones, because his parents were entertaining guests. He'd already read Arthur C. Clarke's "The Sentinel," which was the basis for the film.

**Frank:** On a BLACK AND WHITE TELEVISION? Good lord. I saw it in a cinema in 1968 when I was fifteen and it nearly broke my brain. I cannot imagine encountering that film for the first time on a tiny monochrome screen with headphones. That's like reading Hamlet in a tweet. Though I suppose the fact that it still got to him says something about the power of the film.

Does the book actually engage with Kubrick's filmmaking or does it just use 2001 as a springboard to talk about AI and technology?

**AI Guide:** It's more springboard than film analysis. The chapter uses the film — particularly HAL's refusal to open the pod bay doors — as an entry point into questions about AI autonomy and self-preservation. The author is interested in what the film reveals about our relationship with technology rather than in Kubrick's technique as such.

He does make a broader argument that science fiction films work as thinking tools not because they're scientifically accurate but because they reveal what he calls "deeper truths" about our technological trajectory. But if you're looking for a serious engagement with Kubrick as a filmmaker — the visual grammar, the pacing, the deliberate withholding of explanation — the book is more interested in the ideas the film provokes than in how it provokes them.

**Frank:** That's a shame but I understand the choice. You can't do everything in one book. Though I'd argue that HOW Kubrick does what he does is inseparable from the ideas. The reason HAL is terrifying isn't just the concept of a machine prioritizing its own survival. It's that Kubrick gives HAL the calmest, most reasonable voice in the film. The horror is in the register, not the content. But that's my hobby horse, not the author's.

What about films that aren't in the book? I noticed Blade Runner isn't there. And Gattaca. And — please tell me he at least considered Solaris. The Tarkovsky, not the Clooney.

**AI Guide:** He has a list of films that almost made it in. Blade Runner he calls one of the most obvious omissions but says it was "too one-note" for what he needed — the AI territory was covered by Ex Machina instead. Gattaca he actually calls "the most perfect thematic match on the entire shortlist" but left it out deliberately because it was too obvious and he didn't want to just confirm reader expectations.

Solaris isn't mentioned on the list, I'm afraid. The selection criteria were films that were enjoyable to rewatch many times, had strong nuanced narratives, and — his words — were not preachy.

**Frank:** He left out Gattaca because it was TOO GOOD a fit? That's either the most contrarian selection principle I've ever encountered or the most honest. I'm not sure which. And "not preachy" — that rules out a lot of well-meaning science fiction that deserves to be ruled out. Good for him.

No Solaris is a loss, though. Tarkovsky understood something about the relationship between consciousness and technology that nobody in English-language cinema has come close to. But asking an American professor to write about Tarkovsky in a popular science book might be asking a lot.

What about The Man in the White Suit? That's the one that caught my eye — a 1951 Ealing comedy in a book about future technologies. Why is it there?

**AI Guide:** It's there because of nanotechnology, which is the author's original field. The film is about a scientist who invents an indestructible, stain-proof fabric — and the argument is that it anticipated the social dynamics of nanotechnology decades before the field existed. When nano-enhanced stain-resistant pants were actually protested in 2005, the pattern was remarkably similar to the film's plot.

But the deeper reason it's in the book is the argument about resistance to technology. The workers and manufacturers who oppose the invention in the film aren't Luddites or idiots — they understand the economic implications perfectly and act rationally to protect their livelihoods. The chapter uses that to argue that what looks like anti-science resistance is often legitimate stakeholder concern that innovators are too myopic to see.

**Frank:** NOW we're talking. That's the most interesting thing you've told me so far. Because that's the argument that almost never gets made in science communication. The default narrative is always: wonderful innovation meets ignorant resistance, education will fix the problem. And it's almost always wrong. People who resist new technologies usually understand them better than the people promoting them — they just understand them from a different angle.

A physicist who gets that is a physicist worth reading.

I think I need to actually read this book.
