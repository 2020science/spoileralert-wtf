Hammond is a brilliant, charismatic founder who's absolutely convinced he can manage the risks. **Caleb:** Right but that framing kind of collapses the distinction doesn't it. Hammond had no peer review, no regulatory process, no ecological rationale. But he'd push back on one thing: the book talks about "permissionless innovation," which it defines not as literally bypassing regulations, but as building transformative technology driven primarily by your own conviction that you know best. **AI Guide:** Yes. The book doesn't name your project specifically, but the framework applies directly. The "role of scientists" material in the book frames this as a structural gap: innovators focused on whether something *can* work often fail to consider who will be affected by the fact that it *does*.

**Caleb:** I'm aware of this gap. We've started an Indigenous consultation process this year. It's slow and it's complicated and honestly it makes the fundraising harder because investors want momentum not process. But I know it matters

**AI Guide:** The book draws a key distinction between complicated and complex systems. A jet engine is complicated — lots of parts, but predictable. They're statistically inevitable.

For gene drives specifically, the book flags that a released gene drive can't easily be recalled. It crosses borders, it propagates through wild populations, and the ecological consequences cascade in ways that mirror exactly the kind of nonlinear dynamics you'd know from your doctoral work. The parallel to de-extinction is direct: you're engineering interventions into complex systems where prediction has hard limits.

**Caleb:** Yeah I mean that's not wrong. But the alternative framing is that NOT intervening is also a choice with nonlinear consequences. He's arguing that the confidence required to raise $200 million and pitch a TED talk is structurally different from the humility required to navigate a complex system responsibly. And that most innovation ecosystems reward the former and punish the latter. The question the book leaves you with is whether it's possible to be both — the compelling founder *and* the person who genuinely doesn't know what's going to happen.
